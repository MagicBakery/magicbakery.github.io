Ethics

1. Ethical Goals

1. Ethical Goals

This node aims to define the Quest Society Code of Ethics so that an AI can use it to vet actions and plans.

2. Definitions

2. Definitions

Ethics refers to a formalized set of rules or principles that define what is good and right within a society, profession, or community. These rules are often codified in a visible and objective manner, such as in a "Code of Ethics."

Morality, on the other hand, is a person’s internal, intrinsic sense of right and wrong: their “moral compass.” This internal sense guides individual judgments and behaviors based on deeply held values and beliefs.

3. Quest Society Code of Ethics

3. Quest Society Code of Ethics

3.1 Peace Paradigms

3.1 Peace Paradigms

The Peace Paradigm Ranking is a general rubric defining the highest goal of societal peacemaking. From top to bottom, the rankings are: Celebration, Cooperation, Coexistence, Competition, Control, and Conquest.

The Celebration rank is the highest level in the peacemaking paradigm, where not only do groups and individuals cooperate, but the system itself actively encourages and rewards this cooperation, making it a joyful and deeply celebrated part of daily life.

Cooperation: Here, groups or individuals work together towards shared goals, recognizing the beautiful mutual benefits that blossom from their efforts.

Coexistence: At this ranking, different groups or individuals simply tolerate each other, avoiding conflict but not necessarily weaving together in shared purpose.

Competition: Groups or individuals strive against each other, with peace often being a fragile, uneasy balance of power.

Control: Here, peace is maintained through strict rules and a central authority that dictates behavior, sometimes stifling the spirit of individual freedom.

Conquest: Peace, in this view, is achieved when one group completely overpowers and controls others, a path often fraught with lingering shadows.

3.2 Arcacian Principle

3.2 Arcacian Principle

The Arcacian Principle is: "Let people help each other and reward those who do."

3.2.1. Giving Help is a Choice

3.2.1 Giving Help is a Choice

This principle "giving help is a choice" opposes the idea that helping is a duty.

A helper should not be belittled or pressured to help more than they choose.

By design, Quest Society specifically does not want to discourage people who help even if they seek fame or publicity as their motivation.

3.2.2. Let everyone help

3.2.2 Let everyone help

This principle "let everyone help" opposes the idea that helping is a privilege reserved for people who are good enough.

Quest Society supports this ethical principle by not requiring a helper to join any guild to help. They may just help as themself, and credits are tied to individuals who help, not their guild.

3.3 Skill Ratings

3.3 Skill Ratings

The peacemaker skill rating is an anti-fragile rubric that promotes learning and using higher potency peacemaking skill by highlighting when they are needed.

1-Star level skills or proficiency are good as personal reminders to stay good.

These skills can resolve conflicts among people who already recognize themselves as peacemakers.

3-star skills can work on unbiased strangers, including them as helpers to resolve a conflict even when from their perspective, they do not see themselves as helpers.

4-star skills can work on biased strangers, motivating them to get out of reluctance to help, or at lesat stop causing harm.

5-star skills can work on hostile people who are trying to cause harm.

But viewing peacemaker skills this way, a society could gain anti-fragile resilience where hostility is perceived as an opportunity to use and invent new peacemaking skills toward a win-win outcome.

3.4 Peacemaker Archetypes

3.4 Peacemaker Archetypes

The Peacemaker Archetypes is Quest Society's means to emphasize the diversity in peacemaking to help each person see who they can be a peacemaker.

The Paladin archetype has skills related to physical defense and rescues especially from hostile zones.

Clerics are mediators who restore goodwill and happiness by resolving personal and interpersonal conflicts.

Heralds make peacemaking efficient by sharing information, coordinating quests, resources, and training.

Alchemists make peace by creating abundance through studying and production.

Oracles help people make informed choices by telling the truth and foreseeing dangers. Their skills are used to simulate and evaluate policies.
