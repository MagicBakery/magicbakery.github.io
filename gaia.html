ğŸ§­ 2.1. About

This section defines terms and explain what Gaia Credit is designed for. This section should replace the Design Goals section and Library Analogy section.

2.2. Enable Autonomy

For any philosophy ranked Coexistence or above, if it has a concept of territory, it must have some way to calculate how much resource a person can take with them if they opt-out to be independent.

ğŸ€ 2.2.1. Equity

An equity based solution does not suffer from enclosure pressure. https://x.com/LRRHBakery/status/1950248593794949552

2.3. Lose Access for Violations

A person who harms a space or violate other people's rights in that space would have their Gaia Credits locked up until they make amend.

2.4 Gaia Credits

Gaia Credit is a numerical representation of a person's share of the world.

Gaia Credit would support choice democracy by letting people spend their credit to claim the resource they want. If they used up their credit then they can't claim more. Hoarding is when a person claim more resources than their credits.

2.4.1. Gaia Credit Operations

This section lists what a person can do with their Gaia Credits.

2.4.1.1. Bid

Use additional Gaia Credits to bid for a space when those who want the same space could not come to an agreement to share it by join banking with the normal amount of Gaia Credits.

The bidding operation allows people to outbid others to gain custody of contested space above the minimum Gaia Credit price. This means it is possible for the world to have unclaimed spaces where no one would have credits left to bid for.

In Quest Society, bidding "with Gaia Credit" is the same as donating resource rights to get silver credits, then bid with silver credits.

Since doing so does not give you gold credits, it is better to use your resource to help people to get gold credits to bid with, than just bidding with the resource itself.

ğŸ£ 2.4.1.1.1. Question 1

Wouldn't this encourage people not to donate resource when they know that they only get silver, but the person who uses their resources to quest would get gold?

âš–ï¸ 2.4.1.1.1.1. Answers

When a quester quests with their own resources, they get both gold and silver credits. When you donate resource for a quest, the silver credits that they would get goes to you instead.

A person's desire to prevent others from being able to quest is counteracted by a person's desire to quests for credits to get ahead.

In such situation, anyone who does not conform with the "embargo" to not help would immediately get ahead. So the rules of quest society automatically reward the non-conformers ('cheaters' in the eye of those who want to prevent help) who decide to help using their own resources, and no one can stop them.

As a result, it drives toward the only viable method of getting ahead is to help.

2.4.1.2. Bank

Bank Gaia Credits to another person to manage.

Usually done in exchange of goods or servive. This is how large farms would normally work. The farmer is able to get the area of farm land not by using their personal Gaia Credits, but by using the credits from the subscribers. The subscription contract should specify the allowed use of the subscriber credits.

2.4.1.3. Yield

Let go of a space and get your Gaia Credits back.

This is what you do when you no longer need the space.

Yielding implies that someone is willing to take the space. The Gaia Council can be considered the general buffer/manager that will take your unwanted space in good condition so you can get your credits back.

If the equivalent of a Gaia Council is missing, you wuold need to somehow find someone who wants your space.

2.4.1.4. Lock/Unlock

This happens when you hurt a space or violates someone's right in a space. Part of your Gaia Credit will be "locked" until you make amend.

When you "hurt" a space, your share becomes "locked" when no one wants your space for you toget back your Gaia Credits.

You can unlock it as long as you can convince someone else to take it. The person who takes it doesn't need to be someone from the Gaia Council, but the Gaia Council keeps track of who might want each condition of space to help keep exchanges easy.

If you violate someone else's rights, the victim has the right to use your shares to make amend.

If you damaged someone, it is implied that you are willing to make amend with your resources to heal them back better, or that they have the right to stop you from being able to do any harm unless you could convince them that you would stop otherwise.

From the Gaia Council's perspective, choosing to pay back with Gaia Shares would be considered reasonable enough to conclude that victim is not justified to use your violation as a trigger to demand more or to harm you.

The Gaia Council itself does not get involved in using force to stop revenge against a violator. It only uses force (allow the use of force) to seize resource confisgated by violators.

So if someone attacks you, the option that Gaia Council offers is "We can help you secure this amount of resource from your attacker so you may use that share to rebuild. Do you choose this option?"

If the victim does not choose that option, the Gaia Council simply does not get involved. If the victim picks "revenge" by counter attack, that is a different quest unrelated to the Gaia Council.

3. Infographics (Old: Illustrative Scenarios)

3.1. Captain StarTree

Imagine a group of space explorers find another habitable planet, each explorer has a different idea on what to do. One way to let each explorer have their experiment is to divide the planet so each can experiment in their section.

3.2. Library Analogy

Imagine the world is a library and each volume of space is a book. Each creature occupies a volume of space and needs resources generated from some space to survive.

A creature that is sustainable is like a visitor who borrows a book and returns it in good condition for others to borrow. Then if there are physically enough books for everyone, and no one is checking out too many, everyone can get enough book to enjoy!

ğŸ’³ 3.2.1. Library Account

A person has an account to the library and can see which books they have checked out and whether any book is due to be returned. There may be an allowance of how many books they could check out at once. If someone else wants a book they borrowed, a hold can be placed for them to return it.

ğŸ›ï¸ 3.2.2. Multiple Libraries

The entire collection of books could be kept separately at different libraries, and each library may have their own rules on how many books a person may check out at a time.

âš–ï¸ 3.2.3. Fairness

What happens if a library is not fair, such as awarding special privileges to some but not others? With the goal of keeping books sharable, it would be fair for a library award access based on a borrower's ability to keep the book in good condition. The more delicate a book is, the more carefully the borrower would need to be. Differences in access of this type could be considered fair. Unfair differences would be those based on other kinds of favoritism.

ğŸ“ 3.2.4. Merit

Is there a problem if smart people get to borrow more books? Details like this can be decided by each library. People can be allowed to choose which library they go to. When a library has too few visitors, it cannot justify the size of its collection. In reality, the library would be closed down and its books would be transferred to other libraries.

4. Comparisons

4.1. Not Charity

Gaia distribution is not the same as charity.

In a society that uses Gaia distribution, a person can choose to be charitable to others, but the system itself is not an exercise of charity.

A person who cannot make what they need from their share could bank their share at someone who would make them.

When that happens, the maker is not doing that "for free". The maker gains the harvesting rights until the owner withdraws their share. https://nextdoor.com/p/xmpwGthCS_3L/c/1271152195
